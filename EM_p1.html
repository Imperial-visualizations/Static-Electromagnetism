Gauss's law for electrostatics (differential form) can be expressed as : $$ \nabla \cdot \mathbf{E} = \frac{\rho}{\epsilon_{0}}$$ Where $\displaystyle \nabla $ is the divergence operator. A simple way to visualise this law is through electric field lines. Geometrically, the divergence of a field is represented by the net number of field lines moving in or out of a volume element. So Gauss's law states that the number of field lines moving in or out of a volume element is proportional to the charge density inside the element.
We can see this is true if electric charges act as a source or sink of field lines, as shown in the visualisation to the right.
Consider a sphere of radius $R$ and non-uniform density $\displaystyle \rho $, defined by; $$ \rho = r^3 e^{-cos\theta}$$. What is the Electric field of the sphere in the radial direction?
Solution: We want $E_r$ (Electric field in radial direction) as a function of $r, \theta, and \phi$ 1) Use Gauss Law in integral form; $$\int \int E \cdot dS = \frac{1}{\epsilon_0} \int \int \int \rho dV $$ 2) For the RHS use $\displaystyle dQ=\rho dV$ and hence $$ Q= \int\rho dv = \int r^3 e^{cos\theta} dV $$ where $\displaystyle dV=r^2 sin\theta d\theta dr d\phi$ in spherical polar coordinates. 3) Compute the integral 4) We now deal with the LHS. Since we only want the E-field in the radial direction we only need to consider the dot product of $\displaystyle E_r$ with $\displaystyle dS$. Recall from vector calculus (insert hyperlink?) that for spherical polar co-ordinates, in the $\displaystyle \tilda{r}$ direction, $\displaystyle dS$ is given by; $$ dS= r^2 sin \theta d\theta d\phi \tilda{r}$$ 5)Compute the integral of $\displaystyle E \cdot dS$ 6) Divide by $\displaystyle 4 \pi r^2$ and in the wise words of VJT, VOILA! Gauss's law for electrostatics (integral form) can be expressed as :

$\unicode{x222F}_S \mathbf {E} \cdot \mathrm {d} \mathbf {S} ={\frac {1}{\varepsilon _{0}}}\iiint _V \rho \,\mathrm {d} V$

where $V$ is the volume enclosed by the closed surface $S$.

Geometrically, $\mathbf {E} \cdot \mathrm {d} \mathbf {S}$ represents the net flux of $\mathbf {E}$ through $\mathrm {d} \mathbf {S}$. This law relates the net flux through a closed surface to the net charge contained within.
